Two volumes for the in-between interview round — the one that's production-flavored rather than algorithmic, but stops short of full system design.

The practical-coding round is the one most candidates underprepare for. It's not LeetCode — the answer isn't a clever algorithm. It's not full LLD either — you don't need a UML class diagram or a 30-minute requirements gathering. It's the round where the interviewer hands you a fuzzy production-flavored problem and watches how you decompose it.

The recruiter signal is usually some version of: "less about exact code syntax, more about how you're thinking about it." What that actually means: clarify before coding, decompose the problem, name your abstractions, articulate trade-offs out loud, and write code that survives the inevitable "now add this" follow-up.

The two volumes below exercise different muscles. Volume 1 leans concurrency-heavy — production primitives like rate limiters, caches, retries. Volume 2 leans design-heavy — composition, refactoring, modeling. Pick whichever matches what you need to drill, or work through both.
